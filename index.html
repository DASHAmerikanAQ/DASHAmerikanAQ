You turn on the TV or open Hulu. You go to YouTube or look at magazine covers in line at the grocery store. You scan the day’s headlines. Scroll through Instagram or Twitter for the latest meme. There they are.


The Kardashians are keeping up with you.


Since the premier of their E! network reality television series in the fall of 2007, the Kardashians have embedded themselves into an ever-growing web of platforms that currently shape our individual media diets from print journalism to online social networks. In the process, they have emerged in the early twenty-first century as the first family of popular culture, simultaneously the harbingers of our social decline or the champions of third wave feminism (depending on who you ask). The family functions as a litmus test for a variety of controversial issues, including taste, capitalism, pornography, race, ethnicity, cultural appropriation, gender, and sexuality. The Kardashians remain important for their ability to normalize a matriarchal family structure and transform the traditionally private sphere of the home into their center of business, all while maintaining heteronormative assumptions about the objectification of women.


Just how instrumental are the Kardashians in shaping and/or reflecting contemporary cultural desires, anxieties, and consumptive habits? What would it mean to actually keep up with the Kardashians’ ever-expanding, ever-monetizing media empire?


The 2016-2017 Praxis Program Cohort at the University of Virginia’s Scholars’ Lab learned and implemented digital humanities methodologies to analyze the interconnected media ecologies sustained by the Kardashians. In the first section, we theorize the meaning of the name of our project and its relationship to the family’s Armenian ethnic heritage. The second section considers the politics of taste, the placement of the Kardashians in the history of celebrity, and the ways Kardashian-themed fan fiction reflects conceptions of family and belonging. The final section discusses the DH methodologies we used in creating a data set of every tweet written by members of the Kardashian family, as well as every relevant US Weekly article, by using web scraping, text analysis, and topic modeling techniques. Furthermore, the Kardashians use of Twitter, Instagram, mobile apps, online blogs, and even tabloid coverage work together to engage fans in an unending advertisement for their show. Whether commenting on news events, promoting awareness of the Armenian genocide, or producing extravagant television specials, the Kardashian family has harnessed the 24/7 media landscape in unprecedented ways.


So, what does it take to keep up with the Kardashians? The DASH-Amerikan project demonstrates that only through creative uses of DH methodologies can we begin to keep up and engage with this cultural phenomenon. When we do, we dig past the surface of their heavily curated public image to understand the meaning of the Kardashians to contemporary perceptions of ethnicity, race, taste, and sexuality and the centrality of digital media to their indomitable cultural influence.

The title of our project, “DASH-Amerikan: Keeping Up with the Kardashian Media Ecologies,” contain allusions to the Kardashian family, their fans, and the methodological approach we adopted for research on this complex, dynamic realm of popular culture. The title is admittedly dense, but that’s precisely the point; it requires parsing, as does the Kardashian family’s impact on notions of celebrity, motherhood, femininity, and ethnic identity in America. The title gestures to a variety of intended audiences for our digital essay, which includes academics, cultural critics, and the many fandoms of the Kardashians. As we hope to show, these communities are intertwined and cannot be separated by high and low taste, professed ignorance or knowledge of the Kardashians, or the social issues they choose to care about. While our title may function better as the introduction to a traditional “white paper” than it does as a bit of branding (i.e., as the too-long hashtag #DASHAmerikanKeepingUpwiththeKardashianMediaEcologies), it intends to relate particular meanings to specific groups with varying types of expertise. If you’re an academic, you may recognize certain conventions within the title that govern the assignment of dissertation titles and peer-reviewed journal submissions. Without engaging in too much self-analysis (this is the humanities, after all, where authorial uncertainty can bring everything to a halt) we can still pause to ask what messages our title relays. On the one hand, what does the title intend to relate? On the other hand, what level of self-awareness does it reveal about treating the Kardashian family as a topic of scholarly interest? At the level of intention, a possible, non-literal translation of our title would be: “This essay is about the Kardashians. But it’s serious scholarship. You’ll want to read it.” For those who do “keep up with the Kardashians,” however, there is another, deeper set of messages embedded within the title (and in this case, it is fans of the Kardashians who possess the hermeneutic tools necessary to interpret the text). Those who know about the Kardashians will appreciate a variety of references “piled on” the title, specifically allusions to the DASH boutique where Kim, Kourtney, and Khloé sell their fashion clothing, Khloé’s recently launched “size-inclusive” Good American line of denim, and the tendency for anything associated with the family, from girls’ names to consumer products, to include the letter “K.”



One aim of our project, then, is to celebrate a knowledge of pop culture that leads to a deeper understanding of race, class, and gender in contemporary American society. Equipped with such a knowledge base, the users of our site will grasp that “DASH-Amerikan” is not, in fact, arbitrarily capitalized or misspelled—rather, it is multiplicit compound that opens up interest in the Kardashians to further analysis.


If “DASH-Amerikan” is a metonym for the project, at large, the subtitle, “Keeping Up with the Kardashian Media Ecologies,” simultaneously references the Keeping Up with the Kardashians TV show on E! and the methodological approach we adopted for research on popular culture. Within our study of the Kardashians, this methodology is used to describe dynamics between women in the family, the digital platforms they publish to, and the public discourse surrounding them. Our use of the word “ecology,” then, links us to specific approaches to media studies (including the work of Marshall McLuhan and Neil Postman), but significantly does not posit a level of scientific distance or disinterest in the Kardashians.1 The opposite, in fact! Our project is ecological precisely because it participates in—rather than stands apart from—discussions of the Kardashians found in Instagram accounts, fanfiction websites, and grocery store tabloids. Since the edges of an ecology always remain blurred, we present our research on the Kardashians as a series of insights rather than a collection of detached findings or unveilings of the family’s significance. In the end, we hope our essay reveals the difficulties in doing research on popular culture and treats the Kardashians as a real family who deserves respect and have had a dramatic impact on American culture.


Finally, the dash (-) in the title (and in this case, we here we mean the literal piece of punctuation, not the DASH boutique) also refers to the Kardashians’ Armenian-American ethnic identity, which they express in a variety of ways. As our section on “Distant Reading the Kardashians” hopes to make clear, the Kardashians often reference the family’s Armenian roots in their social media production, either as they “check in” via Twitter at the Carousel Armenian restaurant in Hollywood or attend the movie premier of the The Promise (2016) with Cher. While it didn’t come from the family, themselves, the Kardashians’ Armenian roots were the focus of an article by Will Stewart for the Daily Mail Online (6 February 2015), that details the flight of the “Kardaschoffs” out of the village of Karakale prior to the start of the Armenian genocide in 1915 and their path to America.2As the epigraph by Teddy Roosevelt included above suggests, a hybrid ethnic identity is often denigrated in America, a country that celebrates assimilation and the “American way” over a supposedly "dangerous" multiculturalism. A dash (-), then, speaks not only to how the Kardashians move amongst social media platforms within a sophisticated electronic ecology, but also the way they approach, celebrate, and market their ethnic roots to the general public.

In 2015, Q Scores, the data gathering company that measures the “consumer appeal of personalities, characters, licensed properties, programs and brands,” delivered a report on the nation’s most disliked celebrities.4 Perhaps unsurprisingly to her many critics, the reality television star and media empress Kim Kardashian ranked the highest negativity rating of any celebrity. But the fact that she scored so poorly with viewers did not translate into a fall in popularity for her or her family’s media brand. Q Score Executive Vice President Henry Schafer believed that audiences enjoyed a “love to hate” relationship with Kim and that negativity worked for Kardashian because “That's how she markets herself.”5

This profound dislike for Kim and, by extension, the Kardashian family reflected an odd metric of popularity and taste in general, one certainly echoed by critics of their show Keeping Up with the Kardashians (KUWTK) and reality television in general. Reality TV and its stars have long made easy targets for popular and critical derision. From the drunken fights on the Real Housewives docu-soap franchise to the hixploitation of Here Comes Honey Boo Boo and Swamp People, reality television often traffics in heavily-edited narratives, endless recaps, and outsized personalities that blur the line between stereotyping and engaged character development. Journalist and media studies scholar Sherri Williams has commented how “It seems that reality show directors and producers are not looking to cast whole, complete people. They’re casting types, and that leads to stereotyping.”6 Over the past twenty years or so, networks have offered viewers the chance to watch America’s culture wars play out in the odd-couple pairings of Wife Swap or vicariously fulfill their DIY fantasies on Fixer Upper. Audiences can/should never feel certain that the stories played out before them maintain even the most tangential relationship to reality. Still, it seems network and cable stations have rushed to service an array of niche markets with their choice reality shows, while experts warn of its insidious influence. American popular culture reflects a market structure of an ever-increasing number of outlets for countless programming options, from AMC’s prestige shows like Mad Men to A&E’s Duck Dynasty. In the process, this fracturing of viewing markets has led to TV success on a scale of micropopularity in which the critically acclaimed Breaking Bad can find an audience along with KUWTK, an undoubtedly "lowbrow" production in the taste hierarchy.

Oddly enough, these derisive opinions make Hamm and his fellow detractors sound less like employees of the same media-made star system as Kim and the family and more like mid-century cultural critics. In 1939, art critic Clement Greenberg published his seminal essay “Avant Garde and Kitsch” in the Partisan Review, lamenting that society seemed to be trading real art for kitsch. Real art, specifically the avant garde, conveyed authentic expression while kitsch traded in ersatz sentiment and built-in emotional responses. For Greenberg, kitsch included much of what we call pop culture: “popular, commercial art and literature with their chromeotypes, magazine covers, illustrations, ads, slick and pulp fiction, comics, Tin Pan Alley music, tap dancing, Hollywood movies, etc, etc.” In short, kitsch included all mass produced, mass consumed entertainment, which “pretends to demand nothing of its customers except their money––­­not even their time.”14 Like kitsch, the Kardashians seem to lack positive value, a judgment shared among all the opinions and social science data included here. Through their TV show and glamorous social media presence, the Kardashians mass-produce their own images to create an ersatz intimacy with their viewers and fans, turning themselves into mass-market commodities in the process. All the emotion is prepackaged and built-in. Fans must only turn on the television, open a tabloid, or check their Instagram accounts to consume what so many have deemed void of any value.

Yet such judgments of value are also judgments of taste, and judgments of taste are social judgments. In his study of Céline Dion, Let’s Talk About Love: A Journey to the End of Taste, music critic Carl Wilson explores why people become fans of an artist so patently uncool as Dion. Wilson builds off of Pierre Bourdieu’s Distinction: A Social Critique of the Judgment of Taste, noting that taste judgments are all about establishing boundaries used to distinguish one set of preferences from those deemed to be lower in the social hierarchy. But while Bourdieu drew these conclusions by analyzing the way taste corresponds to economic class, Wilson remains more interested in the cultural capital of “coolness” than any strict economic approach. He understands that holding cultural capital does not correlate to economic class, a fact evidenced by the middling incomes of taste-making, pop music writers (himself included).15

×

Being cool does not always pay well, but at least it delivers the cultural capital of top-tier taste distinctions and with it, a sense of superiority based on what one does not consume. You read The Paris Review, not US Weekly. You watch Girls, not KUWTK. Yet the demographics with the most cultural capital may even engage in ironic consumption of “bad” art or entertainment. As Wilson writes, “Having a ‘guilty pleasure,’ for instance, can be an asset in this system of cultural capital because it suggests that you are so cool that you can afford to risk it on something goofy, ungainly and awkward––which makes you that much cooler.”16 So, maybe you do watch KUWTK, but you do so by “hate watching” as suggested by those Q Score findings. The Kardashians are worthless for so many critics because they hold no cultural capital, no symbolic power. They embody inauthenticity. They are all easy money and no taste. The only way to enjoy them and maintain credibility is by distancing oneself through ironic detachment if you want to maintain a place above them in the hierarchy of taste.


But not everyone who keeps up with the Kardashians is “hate watching.” And if the Kardashians are so vacuous and inauthentic, then what does it mean for someone to enjoy them genuinely? To answer these questions, our work digs past the superficial critiques of the Kardashian family’s rise to fame, their ostensibly valueless presence, and their perceived negative influence on society to analyze the content and context of their media empire. What might we find if we take seriously the idea the Kardashians might be engaged in positive emotional labor for the people who actually identify with them and their image? And what does it say about the harshest critics if they can so easily categorize the Kardashians and, by extension, their sincerest fans as valueless? Through the digital humanities methods outlined below, DASH-Amerikan uncovers the actual content of Kardashian media ecology to work past the easy critiques of, perhaps, the most famous and polarizing family in the United States.


Historical Context of Celebrity, Marketing and Performative Promotion


Kim Kardashian has been noted as a trailblazer in the utilization of 21st century social media technologies for self-promotion, is a consistent practitioner of the “selfie,” and maintains a steady devotion to Instagram and other social media for the promotion of her personal brand and reality show KUWTK. Millions of fans “keep up” with the Kardashian brand through social media, celebrity tabloids, and “entertainment” sections of news websites. The Kardashian empire has encouraged, to a large extent, fan interactivity with the digital brand— Kim Kardashian has over 50 million followers on Twitter and Instagram, which for many creates a personal sense of connection to the Kardashians. The show itself has a strong presence on social media, and fans are encouraged to download and use the “cellphone apps” created by Kim and her sisters.

The status of the Kardashian brand in contemporary American society is impossible to understand without looking back to celebrity and marketing in the 20th century and before, and how modern celebrity practice was influenced by changes in technology and the expansion of the entertainment industry. Promotion of celebrity status through performative practice has existed since the very notion of celebrity itself, and our modern understanding of celebrity according to Fred Inglis’ A Short History of Celebrity dates to the mid-1700s.19 Inglis sees a great difference between modern fame spectacles and pre-modern ceremonies. He notes the distinction between of the Royal Progresses of Elizabeth II and modern spectacles of celebrity, which he states as having emerged in the mid-18th century. Within the context of the development of late-18th century industrialization, marketing and promotional activities vastly expanded in order to reach consumers, with individual entrepreneurs and companies employing varied methods of promotion. While print marketing was commonplace, novel promotional techniques were experimented with in the 19th century— Joshua Gamson notes some of the earliest examples of celebrity promotion, and draws attention to the unique marketing practices in P. T. Barnum’s performative stunts of the late 19th century. 20 I In the 21st century, marketing practices have perhaps taken a page out of Barnum’s book, and become increasingly performative with the encouragement of brand engagement through a variety of social media platforms.

Media reproduction and the photographic and video technology changed the very nature of media itself in the 20th century, as noted by critics like Walter Benjamin and Leo Lowenthal. Benjamin saw the capabilities of image reproduction to be a watershed moment that overturned prior understanding of the nature of images themselves, and had vital ramifications for the worlds of art, media, and marketing. Constant image reproduction, in Benjamin’s view, led to the loss of an “aura” inherent in pre-technological reproduction of images.21

Frankfurt School critic, Leo Lowenthal, noted the changing nature of celebrity in the essay “The Triumph of Mass Idols,” (1944) in which he compared literary biographies of the 1900s and 1910s to those of the 1920s and 1930s. Lowenthal’s finding is that the more recent biographies are focused on stars produced for mass-consumption, heralding a new of type of celebrity whose image is more vital than the actual things that they create or produce.22 The post-WWII era saw a new phase in the refinement of marketing for the promotion of commodities. Indeed, Margaret Mead noted in 1956 “There is not a culture of America, but a culture of Madison Avenue.”23 In this era celebrity and marketing meshed together in a way that blurred the edges between celebrity and marketing. The idea of people who are “famous for being famous” came into existence with Daniel Boorstin’s (1961) take on the state of American culture in The Image: A Guide to Pseudo-Events in America, where Boorstin notably proclaimed celebrities as “human pseudo-events.” 24 Are the Kardashian merely a continuation of Boorstin’s observation, but with the utilization of new technologies? Are we simply experiencing spectacles and “pseudo-events” through new mediums?


It could be said that the public is drawn to the artificial, surface-level aspects of the Kardashian media machine. However, beyond the promotional, profit-oriented aspects of celebrity marketing promotion, do we see genuine concerns in the Kardashian marketing machine? Especially with the utilization of social media in mind, can bits of actual authenticity exist interlaced within a constructed illusion of authenticity? Our corpus of tweets revealed activism for recognition of the Armenian genocide and other socially focused goals. Thus, in their media ecologies, the Kardashian are neither purely promotional nor purely authentic. The same platforms are used for multiple purposes in the Kardashian media landscape, whether they are profit-oriented or otherwise. In 2015, the series premieres of Keeping Up with the Kardashians outperformed the final season of the critically-acclaimed AMC show Mad Men. Somewhat ironically, is there a bridge between the Kardashians and the mid-century advertising executives of Madison Avenue? Has what Margaret Mead called the “culture of Madison Avenue” in 2017 been supplanted by the “culture of the smartphone, the selfie, and the hashtag”? As one of the of the most successful self-promoters using these technologies, does Kim Kardashian have an instinctive feel for marketing and self-promotion in contemporary American culture that makes her more like Don Draper than anyone would have ever suspected?


data collection

This section narrates our process for collecting and cleaning various Kardashian archives, which we produce here to serve as a full listing of our materials as well as to provide information on best practices for these data types. It’s important to note that our efforts were primarily aimed at creating a textual archive, since the tools and methods to explore text seemed more robust to us than any other alternative.


Closed Captioning


We gathered closed captioning transcripts for 11 seasons of the flagship Kardashian reality TV show, Keeping up with the Kardashians. To do so, we used the open-source foot and a the video streaming site Hulu. CCExtractor gave us works with two file types, .srt and .vtt. The .srt files are created by running optical character recognition on video subtitles, much the same way other “OCR” software scans and saves text files from scans of printed pages. The more reliable .vtt format, on the other hand, is a web standard for closed caption files that can be downloaded directly since it is meant to be retrieved by the browser and then displayed on top of streaming video. Both file types have a similar format, however, where the text from the captions is displayed beside a timestamp for the video and the name of the line’s speaker.

While the timestamp information has obvious uses for other data-centric analysis of television or movies, we chose to remove the time stamps from our files in order to focus on the dialogue and speakers. While many of these tasks took time, particularly the process of retrieving each episode, they were relatively straightforward once we had determined the best workflow.

JSON stands for Javascript Object Notation, a standardized format that merges human readability with an easy way to manipulate the data inside. It is composed of a series of comma separated values that are individually tagged and placed within entries separated by curly brackets.

We took a number of approaches to data mining Twitter over the course of the year. Our first efforts used the statistical computing language R, and the package rtweet. This gave us relatively easy to use access to Twitter’s streaming API, allowing us to retrieve recent tweets around keywords related to the Kardashians and even a certain number of tweets from the accounts themselves. While rtweet and the streaming API affords us useful tools for retrieving data on events (for example trending topics) in real time, it was quite limited as a tool for archiving older tweets. It was particularly useful in becoming acquainted with the formatting and metadata associated with tweets downloaded in the JSON file format.25


It soon became clear, however, that we would need to delve further into the twitter archive to explore the data we were most interested in around the Kardashians. We noticed that we could circumvent the streaming API’s limitations by viewing old tweets with Twitter’s advanced search function. Using the browser, it was simple to display tweets by user, hashtag, and most importantly, timestamp range dating back to the beginnings of twitter. While this was an unsuitably halting way to explore very old tweets, it provided us with proof of concept at least; Twitter’s own tools for searching through tweets could be used to find whatever archives we would ultimately settle on. After some diligent Googling, we came across Tom Dickinson’s writeup of Twitter-scraping with Python using the advanced search feature. Dickinson's code was quite easy to adapt for our purposes, and in short order we were able to gather the complete twitter archive of the relevant Kardashians. The script works by constructing a URL from fields that the user inputs (username, tweet text, date range etc) and scraping tweets into JSON files using the Python library Beautiful Soup. The script is even quite speedy; by using “multithreading” to access the relevant tweets through multiple parallel queries to the page, it is capable of retrieving many thousands of tweets a minute. All that remained was to combine the JSON files representing each thread in the command line.

Keeping Up With Topic Modeling and Distant Reading

There’s a lot of (rightful) concern about topic modeling in the humanities, given the seeming opacity of the algorithms that attempt to retrieve semantic meaning. While algorithms like LDA have been used successfully to reinforce or challenge arguments in fields like literary studies, our intention from the start has been to use these tools as a way to tackle the practically unreadable: 13 seasons of KUWTK, twitter presences dating back to 2006, and thousands of Us Weekly articles. In other words, our methods have been from the start about exploring these datasets rather than using them as evidence or conclusions in their own right. As Ted Underwood writes in an explanation of the mechanics of topic modeling, “the practice of topic modeling makes good sense on its own” even when the proof of its efficacy is “brain-squashingly hard”.27 If topic modeling ever becomes a standard of proof anything like close reading of primary and secondary sources, then the assumptions and bias of the mode will be worth considering deeply. However, our exploratory use of the method for a set of data that is otherwise unreadable has given us ample food for thought, pointing the project in a number of new directions we couldn’t have predicted. As our analysis of the KUWTK scripts below shows, sampling topics was only a first step that brought forward material which was best analyzed by the language of critical theory and cultural studies.


That said, we would like to use some of the space of this essay to speak broadly to topic modeling as a method, and justify its use beyond merely auguring the spirit of the Kardashians like an algorithmic Ouija board. One of the most important things to understand about the creation of “topics” from a body of text is that they are sorted probabilistically. While as readers we will eventually ascribe meaning to the clustered topics, the models being created by LDA [explain] are looking for clusters of words that tend to co-occur, considering this measure probabilistically within small text chunks and then the corpus as a whole. The final “topics” of any given run of the algorithm are merely a measure of probably-related words. It can be helpful to think of topic modeling as working in the opposite direction of human readers: while we would move through a document and sort words according to different categories we’d expect to find, topic modeling algorithms work narrowly at the level of text, coaxing out likely topic areas by shared space. Topic modeling these corpora allowed us to move beyond merely finding the most unique words to consider unexpected connections between topics. We would certainly still notice a motherhood-type topic looking for unique words, but wouldn’t have seen the semantic cluster around activisism--in our case, a connection between Armenian Genocide and anti-bullying rhetoric.

We experience and interact with the Kardashians on a daily basis, be it scrolling through our Facebook feed, scanning the tabloid headlines at the grocery store, or even skimming the New York Times. While a large audience engages with these materials, another group rolls their eyes and moves on with their day. Yet they still have consumed. What happens when we play with this distant form of consumption and engage with it as an academic tool? Can we read parallels between this type of consumption and the digital humanities methodology of distant reading?

Distant reading was introduced to the humanities by Franco Moretti and scholars associated with the Stanford Literary Lab. This method of studying literature uses digital methods to aggregate and analyze text with the goal of uncovering the fundamentals of literature on a vast, universal level. It poses itself as the opposite of close reading, which focuses on singular and often exceptional canonized texts. Moretti wrote of the method, "we know how to read texts, now let's learn how not to read them." 28

Natalia Cecire counters Moretti's statement, suggesting that while Moretti's intellectual goals include moving beyond the 1% of literature that comprises of the canon, he is nevertheless propagating notions of canonization. Time equals work, which then equals value. Consequently, through the process of distant reading, one is declaring that one is not willing to put in the work to read a corpus and therefore it is of little value. Cecire evokes Gertrude Stein, an author both part of the cannon and considered "unreadable" by critics: "Distant reading is women's work, and Gertrude Stein shows us why. If distant reading is-avowedly-a compromised form of reading, then what does it mean to distant read Stein's The Making of Americans (1925)?"29 By situating the approach of distant reading "within the history of ways of not reading Gertrude Stein-a history of media, women's labor, and the status of literature" she points to the fear of the corporeal body of the female author of which Stein's literature continually evokes. Stein's body, the source of her labor, "fat, female, lesbian, and Jewish [...] is conventionally seen as the source of her (unreadable, not worth reading) writing." 30


A distant reading of the Kardashian corpus cannot escape these paradigms and contradictions. How can we acknowledge all the types of female labor of which the Kardashian corpus under analysis is the byproduct? The majority of the Kardashian enterprise focuses exclusively on the corporeal form, presenting a sexualized-often exoticized-feminine ideal. What happens when we study this corpus as purely textual? One can argue that it allows the academic, who is predisposed to judgement, a sense of neutrality. Therefore, by removing the visual from this particular equation, we can study the Kardashians on their own terms. It should be noted that distant reading, through topic modeling, was a methodology chosen based on practicality. How does one "catch up" on twelve seasons of Keeping Up with the Kardashians and read every single tweet over the course of 8 years?


Distant reading allowed for themes we would have otherwise not have been able to grasp over the course of a year of binge-watching KUWTK and challenged the dominant narratives about the Kardashians that viewers passively consume. It further allowed us to parse out the authorship, independence and individuality of tweets. It became very clear that whether or not the Kardashian women have personal assistants curating their social media presence, they have each created a unique authorial voice distinct from their sisters. With an acknowledgment that this is not how the average viewer consumes the Kardashians, we have enacted close readings of specific episodes with the aim of rectifying this imbalance and acknowledging the labor intensive nature of television and social media production.

He writes, "[t]he play of identity and difference which constructs racism is powered not only by the positioning of blacks as the inferior species but also, at the the same time, by an inexpressible envy and desire [...] imply[ing] a process of identification and otherness which is more complex than we had hitherto imagined." 4-14

Fanfiction, Fan Response, and Adoption

At several moments during the project we’ve inquired whether the kinds of conversations we've seen in the media presented by the personal and corporate entities of the Kardashians are conversations that are shared by their followers and fans. In order to identify and possibly understand conversational through-lines, the Karshashian's convergence culture, we thought we might look to some of the most engaged Kardashian fans—fanfiction writers. Since the late nineties, scholars have been studying the cultural phenomena of fan fiction, or the fannish practice of writing fiction using the characters and the worlds of beloved books, television shows, movies, and, etc.3 Fanfiction gives us even more ideas about the establishment of a Kardashian cultural community as well as the possible reification and rejection of particular discourses surrounding Kardashian celebrity. Or, what, if anything, are Kardashian fans producing in their position within the Kardashian media ecology?

Fanfiction is generally unpublished in a traditional sense, being primarily shared on the Internet, and before that in science fiction zines, however the bestselling Fifty Shades of Grey series was originally a piece of fanfiction. Karen Hellekson and Kristina Busse, The Fanfiction Studies Reader.

The digital writing platform Wattpad provides a new community of young amateur writers to consider and interrogate. Unlike other fanfiction communities and archives, writers of Kardashian fanfiction produce most frequently on Wattpad. And it is interesting to note that these writers are young, generally teenaged. Unlike other popular fan fiction archives Wattpad operates on popularity metrics of "most viewed" and often publishes their popular author's finished work. The writers' choice of archive and its potential for monetization (Wattpad writer Anna Todd made a six-figure deal for her popular One Direction fanfiction in 2015) is particularly interesting when read through the lens of the Kardashians who always monetize the personal and the amateur. 4

In the tradition of fan fiction studies, we used fanfiction works, especially metadata, as a place to further interrogate the producer-consumer relations of the Kardashians and their audience. In order to begin sifting through this writing community, we built a scraping tool what collected metadata from stories that were tagged indicating they were about the Kardashians. Part of the tradition of fan fiction is a practice of generating effusive and specific tags and descriptions for stories. Thus, it seems potentially more interesting to see how authors not only are writing but also theorizing their own stories. What might we find in collective meta-engagement with individual works?


At first look, the themes within the canon of of Kardashian fanfiction seem to stick to two major tropes: a) the narrator finds she is a missing Kardashian sister and is rescued(!) from obscurity, or b) the narrator is adopted by a Kardashian or Jenner sister. The final scenario is decidedly a "group text" genre in which the writer is imagining what might happen in celebrity group chat. These stories generally feature Kylie and Kendall. It's notable that these narratives misalign with the trends of a majority of fanfiction, the romantic male-driven slash story. [add a link from dailydot]. In fact, without strong tragic narratives in need of "fixing" or male leads to transform into lovers, the Kardashian fandom manages to turn most fanfiction writing practices on their heads.


Wattpad Modelling (20 Topics)

When we turn to topic modeling of the descriptions we see these themes of adoption and communication solidify anew. At 20 topics, relationships, especially familial, present themselves throughout. These themes become especially apparent looking at topics 4, 5, 6, and 8 (which can be found in the modal window above). The topics of fame and family are interspersed throughout, and we see the alignment of Kardashian interest and response to it by fans. The same goes for topics of celebrity and their close ties to family or friendship. The concept of narrative ("story," "reading," "books," "writing") pops up across topics. We see the the influence of not only the act of writing, but also the ways in which the Kardashians fashion themselves as particular kinds of story-tellers (as reality tv personalities, producers of said show, and general content producers). Thus the themes of the show, family, legacy, matriarchy, are mostly reified through their writing fan base.


What we don't see in these fan interactions is the critique that often accompanies transformative fannish writing and fan response. We don't even see the mild interrogations of the tabloid writer or the sharp, quick-witted commentary of the twitter community. For these fanfiction writers, to keep up with the Kardashians is to love them, to insert themselves fully into their lives, to be recognized and accepted within the Kardashian matriarchal lineage.5

Our group drew from a few points of inspiration in considering what form our research on the media ecology of the Kardashians would take. Early in our project development, we surveyed digital humanities scholarship we encountered and admired in the past, including essays using online platforms such as Scalar, Digital Humanities Quarterly, and Vectors.A-1The experimental techniques we found allowed us take some risks of our own, specifically pursuing an interest in projects from the realm of popular culture. In particular, we took an extended look at a digital essay by Will Self, entitled “Kafka’s Wound,” that offers a dynamic and surprising look at Franz Kafka’s short story “The Country Doctor” (originally published as Ein Landarzt in 1917).


Our group sought to emulate the essay, in part, because of its use of “Side-note icons,” or image buttons in the margin of the text that revealed additional, multimedia content.A-2Our team referred to the icons as the author’s “design element of surprise,” and sought to emulate it with our own kiss icons that are linked to asides that explore some aspect of the Kardashians’ presence in contemporary American culture. Alongside “Kafka’s Wound,” we also took a close look at an essay by Carrie Battan and Glenford Nunez, entitled “Mind Control,” devoted to the singer, songwriter, actress, and model Janelle Monáe that appeared in the online music magazine Pitchfork. The essay’s polished look and the creators’ novel approach to the scrollbar—where navigating up and down makes different visual fields appear, disappear, and produce various effects—pushed our team to design a website that could pass just as easily as promotional content for the Kardashians as it could a piece of scholarly writing.

Our team studied the DASH website, in detail, as well as the brick and mortar stores in West Hollywood and Miami, in detail, in an attempt to pick out their representative features. In the course of our analysis, we came to realize that some of its most distinctive elements are typographic (and are represented, below): the iconic DASH logo; the text within the navigation bar; the division of the site into italicized “featured collections”; and the stark footer with white text on a black background:

Other elements are visual, and involve image buttons that lead deeper into the DASH site. In most cases, these elements are highly modular and appear either as a grid, a row of images, or a rectangular pop-up window that lays on top of the previous content. The cropping (mostly square) and editing of these images (soft and warm) gives the site an aesthetic similar to instagram, which we also tried to imitate through our own set of curated images:

The decision to model the design of our essay after the DASH website is an important aspect of our project, since it shows that we are not merely commenting on the Kardashians, but also being incorporated into the digital pathways around them). As suggested, previously, one of our goals in remaking the DASH website on a server hosted by the University of Virginia is to express, in images, that we are far from disinterested in the Kardashians—in fact, our interest is prolonged, active, and searching. Our facsimile gestures to the fact that we are consumers of the Kardashians, like everyone else, and also our desire to “sell” this project to skeptics who are willing to dismiss the Kardashians as vapid or narcissistic, only. With detractors in mind, we treat the production value of our site as a form of persuasive speech (though, admittedly, one that may not be attractive to those we’d like to reach). As a result, we risk a mild “distancing affect” in our intended audience with the hope that every moment they spend on this site will be the most time they’ve spent thinking about the Kardashians and their impact on American culture.
